Arts and Craft

The Antidote to the Chaos of Modernism

While the rest of the country was modernizing buildings and the way of life by obsessing about speed and making the utmost rational design, free from distraction and decor, the Arts and Crafts movement sought to bring back artistry and individualism. This movement was the antidote to the Functionalist and Modern movement that has been dominating the early to mid-twentieth century. It emphasized the importance of craftsmanship and the value of making things beautiful. Forms found in nature were incorporated into design, and inspirations were also drawn from vernacular architecture rather than from factories. Buildings were to be meaningful and an artwork itself, rather than a machine.

The Mission Inn is located in Riverside, California was built in the Mission Revival Style in 1903. Some may consider this hotel an eclectic mix of styles because its boasts of both foreign and regional styles over its thirty year span of building and expansion. This diversity in architectural styles mirror’s its owner, Frank A. Miller’s, own personality as a world traveler and collector of art. The hotel started as a family home, then a boarding house, and ultimately evolved into a the large historical hotel it is1 today. The hotel was chiefly designed by Arthur B. Benton, though various additionons to the hotel were designed by other architects.

Throughout the entire construction process, Miller was active in the projects and sought to not only develop his hotel but to the city and landscape that surrounds it. One way Miller does this is by visually articulating the “charm of early California” with his hotel’s design by using styles that pays homage to the regional heritage. The Mission Revival Style that much of this hotel is built in is reminiscent of the Spanish missionaries that settled in California a hundred years or so before. Keeping close to the root of the hotel, the homey and resort-like characteristics were kept and emphasized throughout the various changes. This hotel became a popular escape from the chaotic lifestyles and cold winters for the the rich and famous, ranging from presidents to Hollywood stars.

The postcard dates from around 1930s, when the Rotunda Wing of the Inn was completed. This addition to the hotel was not designed by Benton, but by G. Stanley Wilson. Though a different architect, Miller was active in the design of this wing and conveyed to his architect that he wanted a modern building that evokes the Californian charm and emit an aura of relaxation and retreat. The postcard image of the hotel highlights the Mission style with its abundant use of arches and the low pitched red tiled roofs. The use of materials, such as white plaster and stucco, make the place authentic to its regional architecture as do the borrowed details like parapets from Spanish missions located throughout California. Details like the careful application of colors, the absence of ornamentation, and smooth plaster were used to create a warm, peaceful, and inviting atmosphere. This hotel’s architectural style sought to be a visual representation of peacefulness and originality, a remedy to the commercialization and industrialization of the Modern world.
